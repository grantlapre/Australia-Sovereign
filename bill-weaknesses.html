Six Legitimate Weaknesses in the Proposed Bill

These are rule-of-law and constitutional risk flags: clarity, proportionality, due process, and safeguards.

The Bill’s reach can extend to online expression and conduct visible to the public, potentially capturing lawful protest, satire, journalism, or academic discussion.

  • Risk: chilling effect and inconsistent enforcement.
  • Why it matters: laws should be clear enough that people can predict legality.

Liability may turn on whether a reasonable person would feel intimidated, even if no one actually does—shifting focus from actual harm toward speculative impact.

  • Risk: subjective outcomes and overreach.
  • Why it matters: criminal thresholds should be tightly defined.

Some defences require the defendant to establish an exemption (for example, public interest or religious contexts), which can weaken the practical force of the presumption of innocence.

  • Risk: increased procedural unfairness in speech-adjacent offences.
  • Why it matters: safeguards should be strongest where penalties are serious.

The Bill expands executive discretion to refuse or cancel visas based on speech or associations, potentially without a criminal conviction and on a low “might pose a risk” standard.

  • Risk: arbitrary or inconsistent application.
  • Why it matters: strong powers need strong review and evidence thresholds.

Automation can produce decisions that are hard to explain, audit, or challenge—especially where the legislation treats decisions as valid even if safeguards fail.

  • Risk: accountability and procedural fairness gaps.
  • Why it matters: decision-making should remain reviewable and transparent.

“Despite any State or Territory law” clauses centralise power and can bypass local legal protections, potentially increasing constitutional contestability and reducing cooperative federalism.

  • Risk: overreach and legitimacy concerns.
  • Why it matters: powers should be proportionate and justified.
